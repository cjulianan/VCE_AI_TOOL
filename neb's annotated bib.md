## Annotated Bibliography Nebiyou (5/27/26)
### 1.	Quintero, S. (2025). Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Large Language Models: Enhancing Applied Economic Reasoning and Forecasting (Master's thesis, Massachusetts Institute of Technology). MIT Libraries: 
#### Quintero demonstrates that while standalone large language models display high baseline capabilities, their reliance on static pre-training data induces factual hallucinations and structural obsolescence in dynamic economic domains. This study provides critical justification for our prototype by empirically validating that injecting external knowledge bases at inference time via Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) selectively mitigates hallucination risk and guarantees data integrity. Crucially for our architecture, the author isolates a core performance trade-off, revealing that standard, unguided data retrieval can introduce semantic noise that slightly degrades raw prompts. The research confirms that the highest factual accuracy is unlocked only when RAG is explicitly fused with structured prompt engineering scaffolds, such as Chain-of-Thought or In-Context Learning. This provides a definitive blueprint for our project: while we use R tools (tidycensus and blsAPI) to programmatically procure localized Virginia economic and demographic statistics, we must enforce a strict backend prompting scaffold to guide the model's spatial evaluation and ensure absolute data precision for county-level field agents.

### Limitations:
-	Study used data from reports (panels) to test accuracy, not live data. 
-	Do we have the packages to support the RAG scaffolding strategy? If a package is found in Python, it can be converted to R
